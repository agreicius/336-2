Proposition 1.15.3. Equality in \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\).

Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
  1. For all nonzero \(q\in \Q\text{,}\) \(q^2d\) is not a square in \(\Q\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. We have
    \begin{align*} q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d} \amp \iff q_1=q_1' \text{ and } q_2=q_2' \end{align*}
    for all \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\) and \(\beta=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d}\) in \(K\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
🔗

Proof.

  1. If \(d\) is negative, then clearly \(q^2d\) is negative, and hence not a square in \(\Q\text{.}\) Assume \(d> 1\text{.}\) If \(q^2d=r^2\) for some \(r\in \Q\text{,}\) then after expressing \(q\) and \(r\) as quotients of integers and clearing denominators, we see that we have \(n^2d=m^2\) for some nonzero integers \(n\) and \(m\text{.}\) Let \(p\) be any prime divisor of \(d\text{.}\) Since it appears with exponent \(1\) in the prime factorization of \(d\) (\(d\) is squarefree), it appears with odd exponent in the prime factorization of \(n^2d\text{.}\) But this contradicts the fact that \(n^2d=m^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. The reverse implication is obvious. For the forward implication, we have
    \begin{align} q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d} \amp \implies (q_1-q_1')+(q_2-q_2')\sqrt{d}=0 \text{.}\tag{1.15.2} \end{align}
    If \(q_2-q_2'\ne 0\text{,}\) we would have \(\sqrt{d}\in \Q\text{.}\) But this is impossible: if \(d=(m/n)^2\text{,}\) then we would have \(n^2d=m^2\text{,}\) contradicting (1). Thus we must have \(q_2-q_2'=0\text{,}\) or equivalently \(q_2=q_2'\text{.}\) It then follows tautologically that \(q_1=q_1'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
