Lemma 1.9.3.

Let \(a\) and \(b\) be relatively prime positive integers. If \(ab\) is a square, then \(a\) and \(b\) are both squares.
🔗
🔗

Proof.

Given a prime \(p\) and positive integer \(a\text{,}\) let \(v_p(a)\) be the largest nonnegative integer \(k\) such that \(p^k\mid a\text{.}\) (Equivalently, \(v_p(a)\) tells us the power that \(p\) is raised to in the prime factorization of \(a\text{.}\) Note that \(v_p(a)=0\) if and only if \(p\nmid a\text{.}\)) It is easy to show, using the uniqueness of prime factorizations, that a positive integer \(a\) is a square if and only if \(v_p(a)\) is even for all primes \(p\text{.}\) Furthermore, it is easy to show (again using uniqueness of prime factorizations) that \(v_p(ab)=v_p(a)+v_p(b)\) for all primes \(p\) and nonzero integers \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
🔗
Assume now that \(a\) and \(b\) are relatively prime, and that \(ab=c^2\) for some integer \(c\text{.}\) We will show that \(v_p(a)\) is even for all primes \(p\text{,}\) showing that \(a\) is a square. By symmetry, it follows that \(b\) is also a square. Given any prime \(p\text{,}\) since \(c^2\) is a square, \(v_p(c^2)=2k\) is even. Since \(v_p(c^2)=v_p(a)+v_p(b)\text{,}\) and since \(p\) cannot divide both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) we have either \(v_p(a)=0\) and \(v_p(b)=2k\) or \(v_p(a)=2k\) and \(v_p(b)=0\text{.}\) In either case, \(v_p(a)\) is even, as desired.
