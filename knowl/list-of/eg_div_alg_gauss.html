Example 1.13.2. Division algorithm in \(\Z[i]\).

For the given \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{,}\) find \(q,r\in \Z[i]\) satisfying \(\alpha=q\beta+r\) and \(0\leq N(r)< N(\beta)\text{.}\)
  1. \(\alpha=2+7i\text{,}\) \(\beta=1+2i\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. \(\alpha=5-5i\text{,}\) \(\beta=2-4i\)
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
Solution.
In both cases we follow the procedure described in Theorem 1.13.1.
  1. Set
    \begin{equation*} \gamma=\beta^{-1}\alpha=\frac{1}{5}(1-2i)(2+7i)=\frac{16}{5}+\frac{3}{5}i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We pick \(q\) to be a closest Gaussian integer to this element of \(\C\text{.}\) In this case we can pick \(q=3+i\text{,}\) in which case
    \begin{align*} r \amp = \alpha-q\beta\\ \amp = 2+7i-(3+i)(1+2i)\\ \amp =2+7i-(1+7i)\\ \amp =1\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus our instance of the division algorithm in this case is
    \begin{equation*} 2+7i=(3+i)(1+2i)+1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that we have \(N(1)=1< N(\beta)=5\text{,}\) as stipulated.
    🔗
    🔗
  2. We have
    \begin{equation*} \gamma=\beta^{-1}\alpha=\frac{1}{20}(2+4i)(5-5i)=\frac{3}{2}-\frac{1}{2}i\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In this case, since \(\gamma\) lies in the center of the Gaussian square with vertices \(1\text{,}\) \(2\text{,}\) \(1-i\text{,}\) and \(2-i\text{,}\) any one of those vertices can serve as our \(q\text{!}\)
    🔗
    Clearly, \(q=1\) is the easiest to deal with, resulting in \(r=\alpha-1\beta=3+i\) and a division algorithm statement of
    \begin{equation*} 5-5i=1\cdot (2-4i)+(3+i), \ N(3+i)=10< N(2-4i)=20\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let see what happens for another choice of \(q\text{.}\) Taking \(q=1-i\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} r=\alpha-(1-i)\beta=5-5i-(3-6i)=2+i \end{equation*}
    and our division algorithm statement is
    \begin{equation*} 5-5i=(1-i)(2-4i)+(2+i), \ N(2+i)=5< N(2-4i)=20\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
