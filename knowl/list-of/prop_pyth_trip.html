Proposition 1.9.2. Primitive Pythagorean triples.

Let \((a,b,c)\) be a Pythagorean triple.
  1. If \(d=\gcd(a,b,c)\text{,}\) then \((a/d, b/d, c/d)\) is a primitive Pythagorean triple.
    🔗
    🔗
  2. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \((a,b,c)\) is a primitive Pythagorean triple.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The integers \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are pairwise relatively prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Two of the integers \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are relatively prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
🔗

Proof.

  1. We have
    \begin{align*} a^2+b^2=c^2 \amp \implies \frac{1}{d^2}(a^2+b^2)=\frac{1}{d^2}\cdot c^2\\ \amp \implies (a/d)^2+(b/d)^2=(c,d)^2\text{.} \end{align*}
    Since \(d\) is a common divisor of \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\text{,}\) the triple \((a/d, b/d, c/d)\) consists of integers and is thus a Pythagorean triple. Lastly, \(d=\gcd(a,b,c)\) implies \(1=\gcd(a/d,b/d,c/d)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. It is clear that (ii) \(\implies\) (iii) and (iii) \(\implies\) (i).
    🔗
    To show (i) \(\implies\) (ii) we prove the contrapositive: that is, we assume that two of the elements of \(a,b,c\) are not relatively prime, and show that \(\gcd(a,b,c)\ne 1\text{.}\) If \(\gcd(a,b)\ne 1\text{,}\) then there is a prime \(p\) dividing both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Since \(c^2=a^2+b^2\text{,}\) it follows easily that \(p\) also divides \(c\text{,}\) and hence \(\gcd(a,b,c)\ne 1\text{.}\) Similarly, if \(p\) is a common prime divisor of \(a\) and \(c\text{,}\) then since \(b^2=c^2-a^2\text{,}\) it follows that \(p\) also divides \(b\) and hence that \(\gcd(a,b,c)\ne 1\text{.}\) The case where \(b\) and \(c\) are not relatively prime is exactly similar.
    🔗
    🔗
