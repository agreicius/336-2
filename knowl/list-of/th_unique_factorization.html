Theorem 1.13.15. Prime factorization in \(\Z[i]\).

Let \(\alpha\in \Z[i]\) be a nonzero non-unit element.
  1. Prime factorization.
    There are prime/irreducible elements \(\pi_1,\pi_2,\dots, \pi_r\) of \(\Z[i]\) satisfying
    \begin{align} \alpha \amp =\pi_1\pi_2\cdots \pi_r\text{.}\tag{1.13.5} \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗
  2. Uniqueness of factorization.
    If we have
    \begin{equation*} \alpha=\pi_1\pi_2\cdots \pi_r=\pi_1'\pi_2'\cdots \pi_s'\text{,} \end{equation*}
    with \(\pi_i\) and \(\pi_j'\) prime/irreducible for all \(i\) and \(j\text{,}\) then \(r=s\text{,}\) and after a reordering, for all \(i\) we have \(\pi_i'=u_i\pi_i\) for some unit \(u_i\in (\Z[i])^*=\{1,-1,i,-i\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In particular, the number of irreducible factors in an irreducible factorization of \(\alpha\) is unique, and the irreducible factors are unique up to multiplication by a unit (i.e., up to associates).
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
🔗

Proof.

  1. The proof is by strong induction on $N(\alpha)$, the base case being \(N(\alpha)=2\text{.}\) (We cannot have \(N(\alpha)\in \{0,1\}\text{,}\) since \(\alpha\) by assumption is a nonzero non-unit.) If $N(\alpha)=2$, then \(\alpha\) is itself prime by Proposition 1.13.13, and we are done.
    🔗
    For the strong induction step, we assume the result is true for all Gaussian integers with norm in \(\{2,3,\dots, n-1\}\text{.}\) Assume \(N(\alpha)=n\text{.}\) If \(\alpha\) is prime/irreducible, we are done. Otherwise, we can write \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\) for some non-units \(\beta,\gamma\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) Since \(n=N(\alpha)=N(\beta)N(\gamma)\text{,}\) and since \(N(\beta),N(\gamma)> 1\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\{N(\beta),N(\gamma)\}\subseteq \{2,3,\dots, n-1\}\text{.}\) By induction we have
    \begin{align*} \beta \amp= \pi_1\pi_2\cdots \pi_r \amp \gamma \amp= \pi_1'\pi_2'\cdots \pi_s' \end{align*}
    for primes \(\pi_i, \pi_j'\text{.}\) But then
    \begin{equation*} \alpha=\beta\gamma=\pi_1\cdots \pi_r\pi_1'\cdots \pi_s' \end{equation*}
    is a prime factorization of \(\alpha\text{,}\) as desired.
    🔗
    🔗
  2. The uniqueness claim is proved by induction on the minimal length \(r\) of a prime factorization.
    🔗
    Base step: \(r=1\text{.}\) In this case we have \(\alpha\) itself prime/irreducible. Suppose \(\alpha=\prod_{i=1}^s\pi_i\) is another prime factorization. If \(s> 1\text{,}\) then we would be able to write \(\alpha=\pi_1 \cdot \beta\text{,}\) where \(\pi\) and \(\beta=\prod_{i=2}^s\pi_i\) are both non-units, contradicting the irreducibility of \(\alpha\text{.}\) Thus \(s=1\text{,}\) and we have \(\alpha=\pi_1\text{.}\) This proves the uniqueness claim for the case \(r=1\) when \(\alpha\) is itself prime.
    🔗
    🔗
  3. Induction step: we assume that for any \(\beta\in \Z[i]\) with a prime factorization of minimal length \(r\geq 1\text{,}\) the factorization is unique in the manner described. Suppose \(\alpha\) has a prime factorization of minimal length \(r+1\) of the form
    \begin{equation*} \alpha=\prod_{i=1}^{r+1}\pi_i \end{equation*}
    and suppose \(\alpha=\prod_{i=1}^{s} \pi_i'\) is another prime factorization. Since \(r\) is minimal, we must have \(s\geq r\text{.}\) Since \(\pi_1\mid \alpha=\prod_{i=1}^s\pi_i'\text{,}\) we must have \(\pi_1\mid \pi_{i_0}'\) for some \(1\leq i_{0}\leq s\text{.}\) After a re-ordering, we may assume \(i_0=1\text{.}\) We then have \(\pi_{1}'=u_1\pi_1\) for some unit \(u\in \Z[i]^*\text{.}\) This allows us to cancel \(\pi_1\) from both sides of the equation, yielding
    \begin{equation*} \beta=\prod_{i=2}^{r+1}\pi_i=\prod_{i=2}^s\pi_i'\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\beta\) has a prime factorization of minimal length (at most) \(r\text{,}\) induction applies.It follows that \(r=s-1\text{,}\) and after a reordering, \(\pi_i'=u_i\pi_i\) for some unit \(u_i\in \Z[i]^*\) for all \(i\geq 2\text{.}\) But then we have \(r+1=s\) and \(\pi_i'=u_i\pi_i\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r+1\text{,}\) as desired.
