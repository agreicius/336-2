Theorem 1.17.2. Dirichlet’s approximation theorem.

Let \(\alpha\) be an irrational real number. There are infinitely many integer pairs \((m,n)\) satisfying \(\gcd(m,n)=1\text{,}\) \(n\gt; 0\text{,}\) and
\begin{equation*} \abs{n\alpha-m}< \frac{1}{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

Proof.

Fix a positive integer \(N\) and partition the interval \([0,1)\) into the \(n\) disjoint subintervals
\begin{equation*} I_k=[(k-1)/N, k/N)\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(1\leq k\leq N\text{.}\) For each \(k\in \in \{0,1,\dots, N\}\text{,}\) write
\begin{equation*} k\alpha=\floor{k\alpha}+\beta_k, \ \beta_k=k\alpha-\floor{\alpha}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Note that since \(\alpha\) is irrational, the \(\beta_i\) are themselves irrational (with the exception of \(\beta_0=0\)), are distinct, and satisfy \(0< \beta_k < 1\text{.}\) It follows that each \(\beta_i\) lies in exactly one of the intervals \(I_k\text{,}\) and furthermore, since there are \(N\) intervals \(I_k\) and \(N+1\) distinct elements \(\beta_i\text{,}\) by the Pigeonhole principle, there are integers \(0\leq i< j \leq n\text{,}\) such that \(\beta_i,\beta_j\in I_k\) for some \(k\text{.}\) But then we have
\begin{align*} \frac{1}{N} \amp > \abs{\beta_j-\beta_i}\\ \amp = \abs{(j-i)\alpha- (\floor{j\alpha}-\floor{i\alpha})}\\ \amp = \abs{n\alpha-m}\text{,} \end{align*}
where
\begin{align*} n \amp =j-i\leq N \amp m= \floor{j\alpha}-\floor{i\alpha}\text{,} \end{align*}
in which case
\begin{equation*} \abs{n\alpha-m} < \frac{1}{N}\leq \frac{1}{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Moreover, letting \(d=gcd(m,n)\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \abs{(n/d)\alpha-(m/d)}< \frac{1}{dN}\leq \frac{1}{N}\leq \frac{1}{n}\leq \frac{1}{(n/d)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Replacing \(m\) and \(n\) with \(m/d\) and \(n/d\text{,}\) we have shown that for any positive integer \(N\text{,}\) we can find a pair of relatively prime integers \(m\) and \(n\) with \(n> 0\) satisfying \(\abs{n\alpha-m}< \frac{1}{N}\leq \frac{1}{n}\text{.}\) To create an infinite sequence \((m_k,n_k)\) of such pairs proceed recursively as follows:
  • Pick any postive \(N_1\text{,}\) and let \((m_1,n_1)\) satisfy \(0< \abs{n_1\alpha-m_1}< \frac{1}{N_1}\leq \frac{1}{n_1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  • Given pair \((m_k,n_k)\text{,}\) pick any positive integer \(N_{k+1}\geq N_K\) satisfying \(\frac{1}{N_{k+1}}< \abs{n_k\alpha-m_k}\text{,}\) and pick \((m_{k+1}, n_{k+1})\) satisfying \(\abs{n_{k+1}\alpha-m_{k+1}}< \frac{1}{N_{k+1}}\leq \frac{1}{n_{k+1}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
For any \(k\geq 2\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} \frac{1}{N_{k+1}} \amp < \abs{n_k\alpha-m_k}< \frac{1}{N_{k}}\text{,} \end{align*}
from whence it follows that pairs \((m_k,n_k)\) are distinct.
