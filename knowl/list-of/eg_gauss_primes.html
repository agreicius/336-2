Example 1.13.14. Gaussian primes.

For each Gaussian integer \(\alpha\) verify directly whether it is prime or not, and compare your answer with \(N(\alpha)\text{.}\)
  1. \(\displaystyle \alpha=1+i\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. \(\displaystyle \alpha=1-i\)
    🔗
    🔗
  3. \(\displaystyle \alpha=3\)
    🔗
    🔗
  4. \(\displaystyle \alpha=-1+3i\)
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
Solution.
Note that \(N(1+i)=N(1-i)=2\text{,}\) which implies (indirectly) that these elements are both prime. To verify this directly, it is easiest to show that \(\alpha\) in this case is irreducible, and this is done using a norm argument identical to the one used in the proof of Proposition 1.13.13. Note that \(1-i=\overline{1+i}\text{,}\) and thus the primality of either element implies the primality of the other. Interestingly, we also have \(1-i=(-i)(1+i)\text{,}\) so these two primes are in fact associates of each other. More on that later. Lastly, the nontrivial factorization \(2=(1+i)(1-i)\) shows that \(2\text{,}\) though prime in \(\Z\text{,}\) is not prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
🔗
We claim \(3\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) If \(3=\alpha\beta\text{,}\) then \(9=N(3)=N(\alpha)N(\beta)\text{.}\) Since the divisors of \(9\) are \(1,3,9\text{,}\) we see that either \(\{N(\alpha),N(\beta)\}=\{3\}\text{,}\) or \(\{N(\alpha), N(\beta)\}=\{1,9\}\text{.}\) The first option is impossible, as there are no integers \(a,b\in \Z\) satisfying \(3=a^2+b^2\text{.}\) The latter implies that \(\alpha\) is a unit or \(\beta\) is a unit, as desired. Note in this case that \(3\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) but its norm \(N(3)=9\) is not prime. It is, however, equal to the square of a prime, as predicted by Proposition 1.13.13.
🔗
We first compute \(N(\alpha)=1+9=10\text{.}\) Since \(10\) is not \(p\) or \(p^2\) for any prime integer \(p\text{,}\) we must have \(\alpha\) not prime/irreducible. To verify this directly, note that if \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\) is a nontrivial factorization, then \(\{N(\beta),N(\gamma)\}=\{2,5\}\text{.}\) This tells us to look for factors of \(\alpha\) with norm \(2\) and \(5\text{,}\) which leads us to the nontrivial factorization \(-1+3i=(1+i)(1+2i)\text{.}\)
