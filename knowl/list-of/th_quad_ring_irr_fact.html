Theorem 1.15.18. Irreducible factorizations in quadratic rings of integers.

Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree, let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{,}\) and let \(\alpha\) be a nonzero non-unit of \(\mathcal{O}_K\text{.}\) There are irreducible elements \(\pi_i\in \mathcal{O}_K\text{,}\) \(1\leq i\leq r\) such that \(\alpha=\prod_{i=1}^r\pi_i\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

Proof.

We will prove by by strong induction on \(\abs{N_K(\alpha)}\) that every element \(\alpha\in \mathcal{O}_K\) can be factored into irreducibles.
🔗
First observe that \(\abs{N(\sqrt{d})}=\abs{d}> 1\text{,}\) and thus that the set \(X=\{\abs{N_K(\alpha)}\mid \alpha\in \mathcal{O}_K\}-\{1\}\) is nonempty. Let \(n_0\) be the least element of \(X\text{.}\) This is our base case for the induction argument. Assume \(\abs{N_K(\alpha)}=n_0\text{.}\) If \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} n_0=\abs{N_K(\alpha)}=\abs{N_K(\beta\gamma)}=\abs{N_K(\beta)}\abs{N_K(\gamma)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
and thus \(1\leq \abs{N_K(\beta)},\abs{N_K(\gamma)}\leq n_0\text{.}\) Since \(n_0\) is the minimal absolute norm of elements of \(\mathcal{O}_K\text{,}\) it follows that \(\{\abs{N_K(\beta)},\abs{N_K(\gamma)}\}=\{1,n_0\}\text{.}\) But then \(\beta\) or \(\gamma\) is a unit, by Proposition 1.15.15. This proves \(\alpha\) is irreducible.
🔗
Now assume the statement is true for all \(\beta\in \mathcal{O}_K\) with \(n_0\leq \abs{N_K(\beta)}< n\text{,}\) and assume \(\abs{N_K(\alpha)}=n\text{.}\) If \(\alpha\) is irreducible, then we are done. Otherwise, we can write \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\text{,}\) where \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) are non-units. It follows that
\begin{equation*} n_0\leq \abs{N_K(\beta)}, \abs{N_K(\gamma)}< n\text{,} \end{equation*}
whence the induction hypothesis applies. We conclude that \(\gamma\) and \(\beta\) have irreducible factorizations. Since \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\text{,}\) the product of these two irreducible factorizations is an irreducible factorization of \(\alpha\text{.}\)
