Theorem 1.10.2. FLT: \(n=4\).

The equation
\begin{align} x^4+y^4 \amp =z^2\tag{1.10.2} \end{align}
has no solutions in nonzero integers. As a result Fermat’s last theorem holds for \(n=4\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

Proof.

First observe that if \((a,b,c)\) is a nontrivial solution to the \(n=4\) Fermat equation \(x^4+y^4=z^4\text{,}\) then \((a,b,c^2)\) is a nontrivial solution to \(x^4+y^4=z^2\text{.}\) Thus the given result implies FLT for \(n=4\text{.}\)
🔗
We argue by contradiction. If \((a,b,c)\) is a solution in nonzero integers, then \((\abs{a},\abs{b},\abs{c})\) is a solution in positive integers. As such the set
\begin{equation*} X=\{c\in \Z_{>0} \mid a^4+b^4=c^2 \text{ for some } a,b\in \Z_{> 0}\} \end{equation*}
is nonempty. By the well-ordering principle, \(X\) has a least element \(c\text{,}\) which means there is a nontrivial solution in positive integers \((a,b,c)\) to the equation \(x^4+y^4=z^2\) whose third component \(c\) is minimal. We will derive a contradiction by showing that there is a solution \((a',b',c')\) in positive integers with \(c'< c\text{.}\)
🔗
First observe that if the prime \(p\) is a common divisor of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) then \(p^4\mid c^2\text{,}\) in which case \(p^2\mid c\) and \((a/p,b/p,c/p^2)\) is a solution to \(x^4+y^4=c^2\) with smaller third component. As this is a contradiction, we may assume that \(\gcd(a,b)=1\text{.}\) It follows that \(\gcd(a^2,b^2)=1\text{,}\) and hence that the Pythagorean triple \((a^2,b^2,c)\) is primitive. Without loss of generality, we may assume that \(a^2\) is even and \(b\) is odd, in which case we have
\begin{align*} a^2\amp=2mn \amp b^2\amp=m^2-n^2 \amp c\amp =m^2+n^2 \end{align*}
for positive integers \(m\) and \(n\) satisfying \(m> n\text{,}\) \(\gcd(m,n)=1\text{,}\) and \(m\not\equiv n\pmod 2\text{.}\)
🔗
Since \(b^2+n^2=m^2\) and \(\gcd(m,n)=1\text{,}\) the triple \((b,n,m)\) is a primitive Pythagorean triple. Furthermore, since \(b\) is odd, we see that \(n\) is even, and we have
\begin{align*} n \amp = 2uv \amp b\amp =u^2-v^2 \amp m\amp =u^2+v^2 \end{align*}
for relatively prime positive integers \(u\) and \(v\text{.}\) Substituting \(n=2uv\) into the equation \(a^2=2mn\) above, we see that \(a^2=4muv\text{.}\) Thus \(2\mid a\text{,}\) and we have
\begin{align*} (a/2)^2 \amp = muv\text{.} \end{align*}
Next, since \(\gcd(m,n)=1\) and \(n=2uv\) with \(\gcd(u,v)=1\text{,}\) we see that \(m\text{,}\) \(u\text{,}\) and \(v\) are pairwise relatively prime. Since their product is a square, it follows from Lemma 1.9.3 (and induction) that \(m\text{,}\) \(u\text{,}\) and \(v\) are all squares. Let \(m=r^2\text{,}\) \(u=s^2\text{,}\) and \(v=t^2\text{.}\) From the equation \(m=u^2+v^2\) we conclude that \(s^4+t^4=r^2\text{,}\) and thus that \((s,t,r)\) is a solution to \(x^4+y^4=z^2\) in positive integers. Since \(r\leq r^2=m\) and since
\begin{equation*} m\leq m^2< m^2+n^2=c\text{,} \end{equation*}
we conclude that \(r< c\text{.}\) Thus the solution \((s,t,r)\) has a smaller third component than \((a,b,c)\text{,}\) contradicting the minimality of \(c\text{.}\)
