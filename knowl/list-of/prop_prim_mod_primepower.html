Proposition 1.4.3. Primitive root modulo \(p^k\text{,}\)\(p\) odd.

Let \(p\) be an odd prime, and let \(k\geq 2\text{.}\) If \([a]_{p^2}\) is a primitive root modulo \(p^2\text{,}\) then \([a]_{p^k}\) is a primitive root modulo \(p^k\text{.}\)
🔗
In particular, \((\Z/p^k\Z)^*\) has a primitive root for all positive integers \(k\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

Proof.

Let \(a\in \Z\) be a primitive root modulo \(p^2\text{,}\) and let \(m=\ord[a]_{p^k}\) for \(k\geq 2\text{.}\) The same reasoning as in Proposition 1.4.2 shows that \(p(p-1)\mid m\text{,}\) and thus that \(m=p^\ell (p-1)\) for some \(1\leq \ell\leq k-1\text{.}\) To show that \(m=\ord(p^k)=p^{k-1}(p-1)\text{,}\) it then suffices to show that \(\left([a]_{p^k}\right)^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}\ne [1]_{p^k}\text{:}\) this is because
\begin{align*} \left([a]_{p^k}\right)^{p^\ell(p-1)}=[1]_{p^k} \amp \implies \left(\left([a]_{p^k}\right)^{p^{\ell}(p-1)}\right)^p=[1]_{p^k}\implies \left([a]_{p^k}\right)^{p^{\ell+1}(p-1)}=[1]_{p^k}\text{.} \end{align*}
We finish the proof by showing that \(\left([a]_{p^k}\right)^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}\ne [1]_{p^k}\) for all \(k\geq 2\text{:}\) or equivalently, that \(a^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}\not\equiv 1\pmod {p^k}\text{.}\) The proof is by induction. The base case follows trivially, since \([a]_{p^2}\) is assumed to be a primitive root modulo \(p^2\text{.}\)
🔗
For the induction step, we assume that \(k\geq 2\) and that
\begin{equation*} a^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}\not\equiv 1 \pmod {p^k} \text{,} \end{equation*}
and we must show that
\begin{equation*} a^{p^{k+1-2}(p-1)}=a^{p^{k-1}(p-1)}\not\equiv 1\pmod {p^{k+1}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(\phi(p^{k-1})=p^{k-2}(p-1)\text{,}\) we know that
\begin{equation*} a^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}\equiv 1\pmod {p^{k-1}} \end{equation*}
and hence that \(a^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}=1+qp^{k-1}\) for some \(q\in \Z\text{.}\) Since \(a^{p^{k-2}(p-1)}\not\equiv 1\pmod {p^k}\text{,}\) we have \(p\nmid q\text{.}\) But then we have
\begin{align*} a^{p^{k-1}(p-1)} \amp = (a^{p^{k-2}(p-1)})^p \\ \amp = (1+qp^{k-1})^p \\ \amp \equiv 1+qp^{k}+\sum_{\ell=2}^p{p\choose \ell}(qp^{\ell (k-1)} \\ \amp \equiv 1+qp^{k} \pmod {p^{k+1}} \\ \amp \not\equiv 1 \pmod {p^{k+1}} \amp (p\nmid q)\text{.} \end{align*}
In the penultimate line above, we have used the fact that \(p^{k+1}\mid {p\choose \ell}qp^{\ell(k-1)}\) for all \(2\leq \ell \geq p\text{.}\) Show this for yourself: you will notice that we need \(k\geq 2\) at some point!
