Theorem 1.14.7. Sums of two squares.

Let \(n\) be a positive integer with factorization
\begin{align*} n \amp = 2^k\cdot \prod_{i=1}^{r}p_i^{n_i}\cdot \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j}\text{,} \end{align*}
where the \(p_i\) and \(q_j\) are distinct odd primes, \(p_i\equiv 1 \pmod 4\) for all \(i\text{,}\) and \(q_j\equiv 3\pmod 4\) for all \(j\text{.}\) We have \(n=a^2+b^2\) for integers \(a\) and \(b\) if and only if \(m_j\) is even for all \(j\text{.}\) Furthermore, when this is the case there are exactly
\begin{align*} 4(n_1+1)(n_2+1)\cdots (n_r+1) \amp \end{align*}
pairs \((a,b)\in \Z^2\) satisfying \(n=a^2+b^2\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

Proof.

We have \(n=a^2+b^2\) if and only if \(n=N(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha=a+bi\text{.}\) Consider now the primes appearing in the factorization of \(\alpha\text{.}\) Since \(N(\alpha)=\alpha\overline{\alpha}=n\text{,}\) we have \(\alpha\mid n\text{.}\) If \(\pi\) is a prime dividing \(\alpha\text{,}\) then it also divides \(n\text{,}\) and hence divides (i.e., lies over) one of the primes appearing in the factorization of \(n\text{.}\) Since primes \(\Z[i]\) lie over a unique prime in \(\Z\text{,}\) we conclude that any prime element \(\pi\) appearing in the factorization of \(n\) lies over \(2\text{,}\) \(p_i\) for some \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) or \(q_j\) for some \(1\leq j\leq s\text{.}\) For counting purposes, we will pick specific (non-associate) primes \(\pi_i\) and \(\overline{\pi_i}\) lying over the primes \(p_i\text{.}\) It follows from the description of primes in Corollary 1.14.4 that if \(N(\alpha)=n\text{,}\) we can write \(\alpha\) uniquely in the form
\begin{align*} \alpha \amp =u (1+i)^{k'}\prod_{i=1}^r \pi_i^{a_i}\overline{\pi}_i^{b_i} \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j'}\text{,} \end{align*}
where \(u\in \{1,-1,i,-i\}\) is a unit. But then we have
\begin{align*} N(\alpha)=n \amp \iff N(u)N(1+i)^{k'}\prod_{i=1}^r N(\pi_i)^{a_i}N(\overline{\pi}_i)^{b_i} \prod_{j=1}^s N(q_j)^{m_j'} =2^{k}\prod_{i=1}^r p_i^{n_i} \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j}\\ \amp\iff 2^{k'}\prod_{i=1}^rp_i^{a_i+b_i}\cdot \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{2m_j'} = 2^{k}\prod_{i=1}^r p_i^{n_i} \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j} \\ \amp \iff k'=k, a_i+b_i=n_i, 2m_j'=m_j \end{align*}
for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) \(1\leq j\leq s\text{.}\) Equivalently, we must have \(m_j\) even for all \(j\text{,}\) and \((a_i,b_i)=(a_i, n_i-a_i)\) for \(a_i\in \{0,1,\dots, n_i\}\text{.}\) We conclude that \(n=a^2+b^2\) if and only if \(n=N(\alpha)\) for an element \(\alpha\in \Z[i]\) that can we written uniquely as
\begin{align*} \alpha \amp = u (1+i)^k \cdot \prod_{i=1}^r \pi_i^{a_i}\overline{\pi}_i^{n_i-a_i}\cdot \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j/2}\text{,} \end{align*}
where \(u\in \{1,-1,i,-i\}\) is any unit, and \(a_i\in \{0,1,\dots, n_i\}\text{.}\) Since we have \(4\) choices for \(u\) and \(n_i+1\) choices for each \(a_i\text{,}\) the result follows.
