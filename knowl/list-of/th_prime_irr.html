Theorem 1.13.11. Prime equivalent to irreducible in \(\Z[i]\).

Let \(\pi\in \Z[i]\) be a nonzero non-unit. The following statements are equivalent.
  1. \(\pi\) is prime.
    🔗
    🔗
  2. \(\pi\) is irreducible.
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
🔗

Proof.

The easier implication is (1)\(\implies\)(2); and indeed this implication holds for any subring \(R\subseteq \C\text{.}\) Assume \(\pi\) is prime. If \(\pi=\alpha\beta\text{,}\) then in particular \(\pi\mid \alpha\beta\text{.}\) Since \(\pi\) is prime, we have \(\pi\mid \alpha\) or \(\pi\mid \beta\text{.}\) If \(\pi\mid \alpha\text{,}\) then we have \(\alpha=\pi\gamma\) for some \(\gamma\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) and hence
\begin{equation*} \pi=\alpha\beta=\pi\gamma\beta\text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(\pi\) is nonzero, it has a multiplicative inverse in \(\C\text{,}\) allowing us to cancel and conclude \(1=\gamma\beta\text{.}\) Since \(\gamma\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\beta\in (\Z[i])^*\text{.}\) The case where \(\pi\mid \beta\) is exactly similar.
🔗
The implication (2)\(\implies\)(1) is not true for a general subring \(R\subseteq \C\text{.}\) As you will see, the key property of \(\Z[i]\) we use is the existence of GCDs and Bezout’s theorem. Assume \(\pi\) is irreducible, and that \(\pi\mid \alpha\beta\) for \(\alpha,\beta\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) We wish to show that \(\pi\mid \alpha\) or \(\pi\mid \beta\text{.}\) To this end, assume that \(\pi\nmid \alpha\text{.}\) We claim that \(\pi\) and \(\alpha\) are relatively prime in this case. Indeed, since \(\pi\) is irreducible, its only divisors are units and associates of itself. It follows that a common divisor of \(\pi\) and \(\alpha\) would be a unit or an associate of \(\pi\text{.}\) But since \(\pi\nmid \alpha\text{,}\) no associate of \(\pi\) divides \(\alpha\text{.}\) We conclude that the only common divisors of \(\pi\) and \(\alpha\) are units, and hence that the two are are relatively prime. From Corollary 1.13.8 we conclude that \(\pi\mid \beta\text{,}\) as desired.
