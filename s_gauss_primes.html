    Section 1.14 Gaussian primes

    Objectives

    1. Give a complete description of the prime elements of \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Describe the “splitting” behavior of integer primes in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Give a complete description of which integers can be expressed as a sum of two squares.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    From proposition Proposition 1.13.13, we know that if \(\pi\) is a Gaussian prime, then \(N(\pi)=\pi\overline{\pi}\in \{p,p^2\}\) for some prime integer \(p\text{.}\) As a consequence, every Gaussian prime \(\pi\) satisfies \(\pi\mid p\) for a unique prime integer \(p\text{.}\) We will say \(\pi\) lies over \(p\) in this case.
    🔗

    Definition 1.14.1. Lying over \(p\).

    For every Gaussian prime \(p\text{,}\) there is a unique prime \(p\) such that \(\pi\mid p\text{.}\) We say that \(\pi\) lies over the integer prime \(p\) in this case.
    🔗
    🔗
    Given a Gaussian prime \(\pi\) lying over \(p\text{,}\) since \(\pi\mid p\text{,}\) it is associate to one of the Gaussian primes appearing the factorization of \(p\) in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) Thus, we can determine all the Gaussian primes by explicitly describing how the integer primes \(p\) factor in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) the so-called splitting behavior of the integer primes.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.14.2. Splitting of rational primes.

    Let \(p\) be an integer prime. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(p\) is not prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(p=N(\pi)\) for some prime element \(\pi\in \Z[i]\) and thus has prime factorization \(p=\pi\overline{\pi}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(p=a^2+b^2\) for some integers \(a,b\in \Z\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(p=2\) or \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We first show that (1) and (2) are equivalent. The reverse implication is the easier one: if \(p=N(\pi)=\pi\overline{\pi}\text{,}\) then \(p\) has a nontrivial factorization in \(\Z[i]\) and hence is not prime/irreducible. For the forward implication, if \(p\) is not prime/irreducible in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) then we have \(p=\alpha\beta\) for non-units \(\alpha,\beta\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) But then \(p^2=N(p)=N(\beta)N(\beta)\text{.}\) Since the norms of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are not equal to 1, we must have \(N(\alpha)=N(\beta)=p\text{.}\) Thus \(p=N(\alpha)=\alpha\overline{\alpha}\) for some \(\alpha\in\Z[i]\text{.}\) Since \(N(\alpha)\) is prime, \(\alpha\) and \(\overline{\alpha}\) are primes in \(\Z[i]\) and thus \(p=\alpha\overline{\alpha}\) is a prime factorization.
    🔗
    The equivalence of (2) and (3) is clear from the definition of \(N\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, we prove the implication (3)\(\implies\)(4) via the contrapositive. If \(p\ne 2\) and \(p\not\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{,}\) then \(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\text{.}\) But then we cannot have \(p=a^2+b^2\) for integers \(a,b\in \Z\text{,}\) since looking at things modulo \(3\text{,}\) we see that there is no solution to the congruence \(3\equiv a^2+b^2 \pmod 4\text{,}\) as is easily checked.
    🔗
    It remains to show that (4)\(\implies\)(1). We have seen already that \(2=(1+i)(1-i)\) is not prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) Assume \(p\) is a prime integer satisfying \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{.}\) From quadratic reside theory, we know that \(\qr{-1}{p}=1\text{,}\) and hence there is an integer \(c\in \Z\) such that \(c^2\equiv -1\pmod p\text{,}\) or equivalently, \(p\mid c^2+1\text{.}\) It follows that \(p\mid c^2+1=(c+i)(c-i)\) in the Gaussian integers. But since clearly \(p\nmid c+i\) and \(p\nmid c-i\text{,}\) we conclude that \(p\) is not prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The next corollary is nothing more than a slight reformulation of our splitting behavior theorem that more explicitly classifies the factorization of integer primes in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) The only additional information is the fact that for \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{,}\) given \(\pi=a+bi\) with \(N(\pi)=p\text{,}\) the primes \(\pi\) and \(\overline{\pi}\) are not associate. This follows from the fact that the only elements of \(\Z[i]\) that are associate to their conjugate are of the form \(\alpha=a+bi\text{,}\) where \(a,b\in \{-1,0,1\}\text{,}\) as is easily shown.
    🔗

    Corollary 1.14.3. Splitting of rational primes.

    1. The integer prime \(2\) has prime (power) factorization
      \begin{equation*} 2=(1+i)(1-i)=(-i)(1+i)^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(p\) is an integer prime satisfying \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{,}\) then there are integers \(a,b\in \Z\) satisfying
      \begin{equation*} p=a^2+b^2\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and \(p\) has prime factorization
      \begin{equation*} p=\pi\overline{\pi}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(\pi=a+bi\) and \(\overline{\pi}=a-bi\) are non-associate Gaussian primes.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(p\) is an integer prime satisfying \(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\text{,}\) then \(p\) cannot be written in the form \(p=a^2+b^2\) for integers \(a,b\in \Z\) and \(p\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    With our explicit description of the factorization of prime integers, we now can give a complete description of the Gaussian primes.
    🔗

    Corollary 1.14.4. Gaussian primes.

    Let \(\pi\) be a Gaussian prime.
    1. If \(\pi\mid 2\text{,}\) then \(\pi\) is associate to \(1+i\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Let \(p\) be a prime integer satisfying \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{,}\) and let \(a,b\in \Z\) satisfy \(p=a^2+b^2\text{.}\) If \(\pi\mid p\text{,}\) then \(\pi\) is associate either to the prime \(a+bi\) or the prime \(\overline{a+bi}\text{,}\) but not both.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Let \(p\) be a prime integer satisfying \(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\text{.}\) If \(\pi\mid p\text{,}\) then \(\pi\) is associate to \(p\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Figure 1.14.5 visualizes the “lying over” relation between primes of \(\Z[i]\) and primes of \(\Z\text{.}\) Each prime \(p\) of \(\Z\) is represented by a point. This prime either remains prime in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) or factors as \(p=\pi\overline{\pi}\text{.}\) In number theory jargon, these two behaviors are described (roughly) as remains inert and splits. However, the prime \(2\) is somewhat special in that its prime factorization does not contain two non-associate primes: i.e., we have \(2=(-i)(1+i)(1+i)=(-i)(1+i)^2\text{.}\) This phenomenon is called ramification (more precisely, we say that \(2\) ramifies in \(\Z[i]\)) and is indicated in the diagram by the funny dot above \(2\text{.}\) Lastly, you may be wondering why our diagram also includes the non-prime integer \(0\text{.}\) This has something to do with notions from abstract algebra (namely, prime ideals). We can make good sense of those dots as well, and explain why they are fuzzy, but that is somewhat outside the scope of this course. We will leave them in our diagram nonetheless as a tantalizing (hopefully) teaser for the abstract algebra sequence.
    🔗

    Example 1.14.6. Prime factorization in \(\Z[i]\).

    Give a prime factorization of \(\alpha=-11+2i\) in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    With a full picture of factorization in \(\Z[i]\) we can now tackle the question of which integers can be expressed as a sum of two squares, Moreover, we will be able to count the number of different ways an integer can be so expressed. The connection with \(\Z[i]\) is the following simple observation: \(n=a^2+b^2\) if and only if \(n=(a+ib)(a-ib)=N(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha=a+bi\text{.}\) Thus \(n\) can be written as a sum of two squares if and only if \(n=N(\alpha)\) for some \(\alpha\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) This norm relation between \(n\) and \(\alpha\) allows us to deduce the possible factorization of \(\alpha\) in \(\Z[i]\) from the factorization of \(n\) in \(\Z\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 1.14.7. Sums of two squares.

    Let \(n\) be a positive integer with factorization
    \begin{align*} n \amp = 2^k\cdot \prod_{i=1}^{r}p_i^{n_i}\cdot \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j}\text{,} \end{align*}
    where the \(p_i\) and \(q_j\) are distinct odd primes, \(p_i\equiv 1 \pmod 4\) for all \(i\text{,}\) and \(q_j\equiv 3\pmod 4\) for all \(j\text{.}\) We have \(n=a^2+b^2\) for integers \(a\) and \(b\) if and only if \(m_j\) is even for all \(j\text{.}\) Furthermore, when this is the case there are exactly
    \begin{align*} 4(n_1+1)(n_2+1)\cdots (n_r+1) \amp \end{align*}
    pairs \((a,b)\in \Z^2\) satisfying \(n=a^2+b^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We have \(n=a^2+b^2\) if and only if \(n=N(\alpha)\text{,}\) where \(\alpha=a+bi\text{.}\) Consider now the primes appearing in the factorization of \(\alpha\text{.}\) Since \(N(\alpha)=\alpha\overline{\alpha}=n\text{,}\) we have \(\alpha\mid n\text{.}\) If \(\pi\) is a prime dividing \(\alpha\text{,}\) then it also divides \(n\text{,}\) and hence divides (i.e., lies over) one of the primes appearing in the factorization of \(n\text{.}\) Since primes \(\Z[i]\) lie over a unique prime in \(\Z\text{,}\) we conclude that any prime element \(\pi\) appearing in the factorization of \(n\) lies over \(2\text{,}\) \(p_i\) for some \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) or \(q_j\) for some \(1\leq j\leq s\text{.}\) For counting purposes, we will pick specific (non-associate) primes \(\pi_i\) and \(\overline{\pi_i}\) lying over the primes \(p_i\text{.}\) It follows from the description of primes in Corollary 1.14.4 that if \(N(\alpha)=n\text{,}\) we can write \(\alpha\) uniquely in the form
    \begin{align*} \alpha \amp =u (1+i)^{k'}\prod_{i=1}^r \pi_i^{a_i}\overline{\pi}_i^{b_i} \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j'}\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(u\in \{1,-1,i,-i\}\) is a unit. But then we have
    \begin{align*} N(\alpha)=n \amp \iff N(u)N(1+i)^{k'}\prod_{i=1}^r N(\pi_i)^{a_i}N(\overline{\pi}_i)^{b_i} \prod_{j=1}^s N(q_j)^{m_j'} =2^{k}\prod_{i=1}^r p_i^{n_i} \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j}\\ \amp\iff 2^{k'}\prod_{i=1}^rp_i^{a_i+b_i}\cdot \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{2m_j'} = 2^{k}\prod_{i=1}^r p_i^{n_i} \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j} \\ \amp \iff k'=k, a_i+b_i=n_i, 2m_j'=m_j \end{align*}
    for all \(1\leq i\leq r\text{,}\) \(1\leq j\leq s\text{.}\) Equivalently, we must have \(m_j\) even for all \(j\text{,}\) and \((a_i,b_i)=(a_i, n_i-a_i)\) for \(a_i\in \{0,1,\dots, n_i\}\text{.}\) We conclude that \(n=a^2+b^2\) if and only if \(n=N(\alpha)\) for an element \(\alpha\in \Z[i]\) that can we written uniquely as
    \begin{align*} \alpha \amp = u (1+i)^k \cdot \prod_{i=1}^r \pi_i^{a_i}\overline{\pi}_i^{n_i-a_i}\cdot \prod_{j=1}^s q_j^{m_j/2}\text{,} \end{align*}
    where \(u\in \{1,-1,i,-i\}\) is any unit, and \(a_i\in \{0,1,\dots, n_i\}\text{.}\) Since we have \(4\) choices for \(u\) and \(n_i+1\) choices for each \(a_i\text{,}\) the result follows.
    🔗
