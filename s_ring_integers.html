    Section 1.15 Quadratic rings of integers

    Subsection 1.15.1 Quadratic extensions of \(\Q\)

    Definition 1.15.1. Quadratic extension of \(\Q\).

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree integer. We define \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) to be the following subset of \(\C\text{:}\)
    \begin{align} \Q(\sqrt{d}) \amp = \{z\in \C\mid z=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d} \text{ for some } q_1,q_2\in \Q\}\text{.}\tag{1.15.1} \end{align}
    We call \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) a quadratic field extension of \(\Q\text{,}\) or a quadratic subfield of \(\C\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The quadratic field \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) is real if \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\subseteq \R\) (i.e., if \(d\geq 0\)) and imaginary otherwise (i.e., if \(d< 0\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.15.2. Quadratic field extensions.

    You will likely notice the similarity in notation between quadratic fields \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) and the Gaussian integers \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) Notice further some important differences: the \(\Q\) in the first notation is an indication that the set consists of \(Q\)-linear combinations \(q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\text{,}\) \(q_1,q_2\in \Q\) as opposed to \(\Z\)-linear combinations \(a+b\sqrt{d}\text{,}\) \(a,b\in \Z\text{.}\) Moreover, the use of parentheses in \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) as opposed to brackets (as in \(\Z[i]\)) is a notational indication that \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) is a field and not just a ring. (See Theorem 1.15.7.)
    🔗
    The quadratic fields \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\) can be thought of as generalizations of \(\Q\text{.}\) The analog of \(\Z\) in these settings is given by the ring of integers \(\mathcal{O}_K\) defined below.
    🔗
    🔗
    You might be wondering about the importance of this squarefree condition. This will arise at various points in our discussion. Our first example is the simple characterization of equality in \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\) given below.
    🔗

    Proposition 1.15.3. Equality in \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\).

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
    1. For all nonzero \(q\in \Q\text{,}\) \(q^2d\) is not a square in \(\Q\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We have
      \begin{align*} q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d} \amp \iff q_1=q_1' \text{ and } q_2=q_2' \end{align*}
      for all \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\) and \(\beta=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d}\) in \(K\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. If \(d\) is negative, then clearly \(q^2d\) is negative, and hence not a square in \(\Q\text{.}\) Assume \(d> 1\text{.}\) If \(q^2d=r^2\) for some \(r\in \Q\text{,}\) then after expressing \(q\) and \(r\) as quotients of integers and clearing denominators, we see that we have \(n^2d=m^2\) for some nonzero integers \(n\) and \(m\text{.}\) Let \(p\) be any prime divisor of \(d\text{.}\) Since it appears with exponent \(1\) in the prime factorization of \(d\) (\(d\) is squarefree), it appears with odd exponent in the prime factorization of \(n^2d\text{.}\) But this contradicts the fact that \(n^2d=m^2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The reverse implication is obvious. For the forward implication, we have
      \begin{align} q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d} \amp \implies (q_1-q_1')+(q_2-q_2')\sqrt{d}=0 \text{.}\tag{1.15.2} \end{align}
      If \(q_2-q_2'\ne 0\text{,}\) we would have \(\sqrt{d}\in \Q\text{.}\) But this is impossible: if \(d=(m/n)^2\text{,}\) then we would have \(n^2d=m^2\text{,}\) contradicting (1). Thus we must have \(q_2-q_2'=0\text{,}\) or equivalently \(q_2=q_2'\text{.}\) It then follows tautologically that \(q_1=q_1'\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.15.4. Quadratic norm and conjugate.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be squarefree, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) For any \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\in K\text{,}\) we define its conjugate \(\overline{\alpha}\) and norm \(N_K(\alpha)\) as follows:
    \begin{align*} \overline{\alpha} \amp =q_1-q_2\sqrt{d}\amp N_K(\alpha)\amp=\alpha\overline{\alpha}=q_1^2-q_2^2\sqrt{d}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We call the resulting function \(N_K\colon K\rightarrow \Q\) the norm function of \(K\) over \(\Q\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Warning 1.15.5.

    It is important not to conflate the general conjugate and norm operations of a quadratic field with complex conjugation and the Gaussian norm function.
    🔗
    Take quadratic field \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2})\text{,}\) for example. Given any \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{2}\in K\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \overline{\alpha} \amp = q_1-q_2\sqrt{2} \amp N_K(\alpha)=q_1^2-2q_2^2\text{.} \end{align*}
    There operations are clearly different than complex conjugation and the Gaussian norm. For instance we have \(\overline{3+2\sqrt{2}}=3-2\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) whereas the complex conjugate of \(3+2\sqrt{2}\) is itself (since it is real)! Furthermore, note that \(N_K\) can assume negative values (\(N_K(2+3\sqrt{2})=4-18=-14\)), whereas the Gaussian norm is a nonnegative function.
    🔗
    That said, in the case where \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\) is imaginary (i.e., \(d< 0\)), then the quadratic conjugate and norm operations are in fact identical to complex conjugation and modulus squared.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 1.15.6. Quadratic conjugates and norms.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) As usual, given \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\text{,}\) we define \(\overline{\alpha}=q_1-q_2\sqrt{d}\text{.}\)
    1. For all \(\alpha,\beta\in K\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align} \overline{\alpha\beta} \amp =\overline{\alpha}\, \overline{\beta}\tag{1.15.3}\\ \overline{\alpha+\beta} \amp = \overline{\alpha}+\overline{\beta} \tag{1.15.4}\\ N_K(\alpha\beta) \amp =N_K(\alpha)N_K(\beta)\text{.}\tag{1.15.5} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Given \(\alpha\in K\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align} \alpha\in \Q \amp \iff \overline{\alpha}=\alpha\tag{1.15.6}\\ N_K(\alpha)=0\amp \iff \alpha=0 \text{.}\tag{1.15.7} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. These are simple algebraic arguments left to the reader.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Observe first that \(\Q=\{\alpha\in \Q(\sqrt{d})\mid \alpha=q+0\sqrt{d}\}\text{.}\) Now given \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \alpha=\overline{\alpha} \amp \iff q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}=q_1-q_2\sqrt{d}\\ \amp \iff q_1=q_1 \text{ and } q_2=-q_2 \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/prop_quad_ext_equality.html}{\text{1.15.3}})\\ \amp \iff q_2=0\\ \amp \iff \alpha\in \Q\text{.} \end{align*}
      For the second part, given \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align} N_K(\alpha)=0 \amp \iff q_1^2-q_2^2d=0 \tag{1.15.8}\\ \amp \iff q_1^2=q_2^2d\tag{1.15.9}\\ \amp \iff q_1=q_2=0 \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/prop_quad_ext_equality.html}{\text{1.15.3}})\tag{1.15.10}\\ \amp \iff \alpha=0\text{.}\tag{1.15.11} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.15.7. Quadratic extensions of \(\Q\) are fields.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
    1. \(K\) is a subring of \(\C\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Every nonzero element of \(K\) has a multiplicative inverse: i.e., \(K\) is a field. More explicitly, given nonzero \(\alpha=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\in K\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align} \alpha^{-1} \amp =\frac{q_1}{q_1^2-q_2^2d}-\frac{q_2}{q_1^2-q_2^2d}=\frac{\overline{\alpha}}{N_K(\alpha)}\text{.}\tag{1.15.12} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. We need only show that \(K\) contains \(0\) and \(1\text{,}\) and is closed under addition, additive inverses (\(-\alpha\)), and multiplication. These are simple algebraic arguments left to the reader.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. This is an immediate consequence of the fact that \(\alpha\ne 0\) implies \(N_K(\alpha)\ne 0\) and the following algebra:
      \begin{align*} \alpha\cdot \frac{\alpha}{N_K(\alpha)} \amp = \frac{\alpha\overline{\alpha}}{N_K(\alpha)}\\ \amp =\frac{N_K(\alpha)}{N_K(\alpha)}\\ \amp = 1\text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.15.2 Ring of integers

    Definition 1.15.8. Monic polynomial.

    Let \(R\) be a ring. A polynomial \(f\in R[x]\) is monic if its leading coefficient is 1: i.e., if \(f(x)=\anmonic\text{,}\) with \(a_i\in R\) for all \(0\leq i\leq n-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.15.9. Algebraic integers of \(\C\).

    A complex number \(\alpha\in \C\) is an algebraic integer if there is a monic polynomial \(f\in \Z[x]\) such that \(f(\alpha)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.15.10. Algebraic versus algebraic integer.

    Recall that an element \(\alpha\in \Q\) is algebraic if there is a polynomial \(f\in \Q[x]\) such that \(f(\alpha)=0\text{.}\) The condition for being an algebraic integer is stricter on two counts: we must be able to find a polynomial not just with rational coefficients, but integer ones; furthermore, the polynomial must be monic.
    🔗
    Note in this regard that any algebraic element \(\alpha\in \C\) will satisfy a polynomial with integer coefficients, just by clearing denominators in the polynomial \(f\in \Q[x]\) satisfying \(f(\alpha)=0\text{.}\) For example, the element \(\alpha=\frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}\) satisfies the monic rational polynomial \(f(x)=x^2-2/9\) and the integer polynomial \(g(x)=9x^2-2\text{.}\) However, we will be able to show that \(\alpha\) does not satisfy a monic integer polynomial.
    🔗
    🔗
    We will primarily be interested in the algebraic integers lying within one of our quadratic fields \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) Before we get there, we assemble some general results about algebraic integers.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.15.11. Algebraic integers form a ring.

    Let \(\mathcal{O}\) be the set of all algebraic integers in \(\C\text{:}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{O}=\{\alpha\in \C\mid f(\alpha)=0 \text{ for some monic } f\in \Z[x]\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    1. \(\mathcal{O}\cap \Q=\Z\text{.}\) In other words, all integers are algebraic integers, and furthermore, the set of rational numbers that are algebraic integers is precisely \(\Z\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\mathcal{O}\) is closed under addition and multiplication: i.e., we have
      \begin{align*} \alpha,\beta\in \mathcal{O} \amp \implies \alpha+\beta, \alpha\beta\in \mathcal{O}\text{.} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    As a result, \(\mathcal{O}\) is a subring of \(\C\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. If \(n\in \Z\text{,}\) then \(f(n)=0\) for the monic integer polynomial \(f(x)=x-n\text{.}\) Thus \(\Z\subseteq \mathcal{O}\cap \Q\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Conversely, suppose \(q\in \mathcal{O}\cap \Q\text{.}\) Let \(f(x)=\anmonic\) be a monic polynomial satisfying \(f(q)=0\text{.}\) Writing \(q=r/s\) with \(\gcd(r,s)=1\) and clearing denominators in the equation \(f(r/s)=0\text{,}\) we see that
      \begin{equation*} r^n+a_{n-1}r^{n-1}s+\cdots +a_1rs^{n-1}+a_0s^n=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since all constants above are integers, we see that \(s\mid r^n\text{,}\) a contradiction since \(\gcd(r,s)=1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. As intuitive as this property might sound, we resort to a little linear algebra trickery to prove it. Namely, we first prove the following sufficient condition for an element \(\alpha\in \C\) to be an algebraic integer: if there is a set \(\{\theta_1,\theta_2,\dots, \theta_k\}\) of complex numbers, not all zero, such that for all \(1\leq j\leq k\) we have
      \begin{equation*} \alpha\theta_j=\sum_{i=1}^kb_{ij}\theta_i \end{equation*}
      for some integer constants \(b_{ij}\in \Z\text{,}\) then \(\alpha\) is an algebraic integer.
      🔗
      Before proving this claim, let’s see why it yields the desired result. Suppose that \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are algebraic integers satisfying \(f(\alpha)=0\) and \(g(\beta)=0\text{,}\) where \(f(x)=\anmonic\) and \(g(x)=x^m+b_{m-1}x^{m-1}+\cdots +b_1x+b_0\) are monic integer polynomials. Since
      \begin{align*} \alpha^n \amp =-\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}a_i\alpha^{i}\\ \beta^m \amp = -\sum_{i=0}^{m-1}b_i\beta^{i}\text{,} \end{align*}
      it follows that the set
      \begin{equation*} X=\{\alpha^{i}\beta^{j}\mid 0\leq i\leq n-1, 0\leq j\leq m-1\} \end{equation*}
      satisfies the condition above for \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{.}\) Indeed, we have
      \begin{align*} \alpha (\alpha^i\beta^j) \amp = \begin{cases} \alpha^{i+1}\beta^j\amp \text{if } 0\leq i\leq n-2\\ -\sum_{\ell=0}^{n-1}a_{\ell}\alpha^\ell \beta^j\amp \text{if } i=n-1 \end{cases}\\ \beta (\alpha^i\beta^j) \amp = \begin{cases} \alpha^{i}\beta^{j+1}\amp \text{if } 0\leq j\leq m-2\\ -\sum_{\ell=0}^{m-1}b_{\ell}\alpha^i \beta^\ell \amp \text{if } j=m-1 \text{.}\end{cases} \end{align*}
      But then it follows easily that \(\alpha+\beta\) and \(\alpha\beta\) also satisfy the claims condition with respect to \(X\text{,}\) showing that these elements are also algebraic integers.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    With these generalities out of the way, we can now define the ring of integers of a quadratic field \(\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.15.12. Quadratic ring of integers.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be squarefree, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) The ring of integers of \(K\text{,}\) denoted \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is the set of all elements of \(K\) that are algebraic integers: i.e.,
    \begin{align} \mathcal{O}_K \amp = \{\alpha\in K\mid f(\alpha)=0 \text{ for some monic } f\in \Z[x]\}\text{.}\tag{1.15.13} \end{align}
    We call \(\mathcal{O}_K\) a quadratic ring of integers.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.15.13. Quadratic ring of integers.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
    1. \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is a subring of \(\C\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Given \(\alpha\in K\text{,}\) we have \(\alpha\in \mathcal{O}_K\) if and only if \(\alpha+\overline{\alpha}\in \mathcal{O}_K\) and \(\alpha\overline{\alpha}\in \mathcal{O}_K\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. We have
      \begin{align*} \mathcal{O}_K \amp =\begin{cases} \Z[\sqrt{d}]=\{a+b\sqrt{d}\mid a,b\in \Z\} \amp \text{if } d\equiv 2,3\pmod 4\\ \Z[\omega]=\{a+b\omega\mid a,b\in \Z\}, \text{ where } \omega=\tfrac{1}{2}+\tfrac{\sqrt{d}}{2} \amp \text{if } d\equiv 1\pmod 4 \text{.}\end{cases} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.15.14. Norm Euclidean.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be squarefree, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) The norm \(N_K\) satisfies the division algorithm if given \(\alpha,\beta\in \mathcal{O}_K\) with \(\beta\ne 0\text{,}\) there exist \(\gamma,\delta\in \mathcal{O}_K\) satisfying the following conditions.
    1. \(\alpha=\gamma\beta+\delta\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(0\leq \abs{N_K(\delta)}\leq \abs{N_K(\beta)}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    We also say that \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is norm Euclidean in this case.
    🔗
    🔗
    The importance of a quadratic ring of integer \(\mathcal{O}_K\) being norm-Euclidean is that we can perform a version of the Euclidean algorithm (Theorem 1.13.5) using \(\abs{N_K}\) to compute GCDs of elements \(\alpha, \beta\in \mathcal{O}_K\text{,}\) and moreover, express a GCD in the form \(\gamma\alpha+\delta\beta\) as in Bezout’s theorem. As we saw in the discussion of \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) that fact then leads to a cascade of useful properties we used to classify the prime elements of \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) As a result, these same properties hold for a norm-Euclidean \(\mathcal{O}_K\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 1.15.15. Norm-Euclidean rings of integers.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1}\) be squarefree, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) If \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is norm-Euclidean then it enjoys the following properties.
    1. Let \(\alpha,\beta\in \mathcal{O}_K\) and assume \(\alpha\ne 0\) or \(\beta\ne 0\text{.}\) There is a GCD \(\delta\) of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{,}\) and we have \(\delta=\gamma \alpha+\epsilon \beta\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. An element \(\pi\in \mathcal{O}_K\) is prime if and only if it is irreducible.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(\alpha\in \mathcal{O}_K\) is a nonzero non-unit, then it has a factorization \(\alpha=\prod_{i=1}^r\pi_i\text{,}\) and this factorization is unique in the sense of Theorem 1.13.15.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
