    Section 1.13 Factorization in Gaussian integers

    Subsection 1.13.1 Division and Euclidean algorithms

    Theorem 1.13.1. Division algorithm in \(\Z[i]\).

    Let \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) be Gaussian integers with \(\beta\ne 0\text{.}\) There are Gaussian integers \(q,r\in \Z[i]\) satisfying the following conditions.
    1. \(\alpha=q\beta+r\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(0\leq N(r)< N(\beta)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Moreover, \(q\) and \(r\) can be produced via the following procedure.
    1. Find \(\gamma\in \Q[i]\) satisfying \(\gamma\beta=\alpha\text{:}\) i.e., set \(\gamma=\beta^{-1}\alpha\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Write \(\gamma=q+(s+ti)\text{,}\) where \(q\in \Z[i]\) and \(\{\abs{s},\abs{t}\}\subseteq [0,1/2]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. We have \(\alpha=q\beta+r\text{,}\) where \(r=\alpha-q\beta\) satisfies \(0\leq N(r)< N(\beta)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    That the procedure above produces a \(q\) and \(r\) in \(\Z[i]\) satisfying \(\alpha=q\beta+r\) is clear. It remains only to show that \(0\leq N(r)< N(\beta)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{align*} N(r) \amp =N(\alpha-q\beta)\\ \amp = N(\gamma\beta-q\beta)\\ \amp =N(\gamma-q)N(\beta)\\ \amp =N(s+ti)N(\beta)\\ \amp =(s^2+t^2)N(\beta)\\ \amp < N(\beta) \amp (s^2+t^2\leq \tfrac{1}{4}+\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{1}{2}) \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.13.2. Division algorithm in \(\Z[i]\).

    For the given \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{,}\) find \(q,r\in \Z[i]\) satisfying \(\alpha=q\beta+r\) and \(0\leq N(r)< N(\beta)\text{.}\)
    1. \(\alpha=2+7i\text{,}\) \(\beta=1+2i\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\alpha=5-5i\text{,}\) \(\beta=2-4i\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    In both cases we follow the procedure described in Theorem 1.13.1.
    1. Set
      \begin{equation*} \gamma=\beta^{-1}\alpha=\frac{1}{5}(1-2i)(2+7i)=\frac{16}{5}+\frac{3}{5}i\text{.} \end{equation*}
      We pick \(q\) to be a closest Gaussian integer to this element of \(\C\text{.}\) In this case we can pick \(q=3+i\text{,}\) in which case
      \begin{align*} r \amp = \alpha-q\beta\\ \amp = 2+7i-(3+i)(1+2i)\\ \amp =2+7i-(1+7i)\\ \amp =1\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus our instance of the division algorithm in this case is
      \begin{equation*} 2+7i=(3+i)(1+2i)+1\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Note that we have \(N(1)=1< N(\beta)=5\text{,}\) as stipulated.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We have
      \begin{equation*} \gamma=\beta^{-1}\alpha=\frac{1}{20}(2+4i)(5-5i)=\frac{3}{2}-\frac{1}{2}i\text{.} \end{equation*}
      In this case, since \(\gamma\) lies in the center of the Gaussian square with vertices \(1\text{,}\) \(2\text{,}\) \(1-i\text{,}\) and \(2-i\text{,}\) any one of those vertices can serve as our \(q\text{!}\)
      🔗
      Clearly, \(q=1\) is the easiest to deal with, resulting in \(r=\alpha-1\beta=3+i\) and a division algorithm statement of
      \begin{equation*} 5-5i=1\cdot (2-4i)+(3+i), \ N(3+i)=10< N(2-4i)=20\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Let see what happens for another choice of \(q\text{.}\) Taking \(q=1-i\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} r=\alpha-(1-i)\beta=5-5i-(3-6i)=2+i \end{equation*}
      and our division algorithm statement is
      \begin{equation*} 5-5i=(1-i)(2-4i)+(2+i), \ N(2+i)=5< N(2-4i)=20\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.13.3. Greatest common divisor and least common multiple.

    Let \(R\) be a subring of \(\C\text{.}\) Given elements \(\alpha, \beta\in R\) a greatest common divisor (or GCD) of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) is an element \(\delta\in R\) satisfying the following conditions:
    1. \(\delta\mid \alpha\) and \(\delta\mid \beta\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\delta'\in R\) satisfies \(\delta'\mid \alpha\) and \(\delta'\mid \beta\text{,}\) then \(\delta'\mid \delta\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    A least common multiple (or LCM) of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) is an element \(\mu\in R\) satisfying the following conditions:
    1. \(\alpha\mid \mu\) and \(\beta\mid \mu\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\mu'\in R\) satisfies \(\alpha\mid \mu'\) and \(\beta\mid \mu'\text{,}\) then \(\mu\mid \mu'\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    More generally, a greatest common divisor of elements \(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\) of \(R\) is an element \(\delta\in R\) such that \(\delta\mid \alpha_i\) for all \(i\text{,}\) and if \(\delta'\in R\) satisfies \(\delta'\mid \alpha_i\) for all \(i\text{,}\) then \(\delta'\mid \delta\text{.}\) Lastly, we say that elements \(\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\dots, \alpha_n\) are relatively prime if they have \(1\) as a GCD.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.13.4. GCDs and LCDs.

    Although the definition of GCDs and LCMs is a straightforward generalization of the corresponding notion from the integers, you should proceed with caution when making use of these concepts. In particular, be aware of the following facts:
    • GCDs and LCMs need not exist in an arbitrary subring of \(\C\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • if GCDs and LCMs do exist, they are only unique modulo multiplication by a unit.
      🔗
      🔗
    The uniqueness issue is already in evidence in the context of the integers. To nonzero integers \(a, b\) will always have two GCDs, namely \(\gcd(a,b)\) and \(-\gcd(a,b)\text{,}\) where \(\gcd(a,b)\text{,}\) as you will recall, is defined to be the greatest positive integer dividing \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.13.5. Euclidean algorithm in \(\Z[i]\).

    Given elements \(\alpha,\beta\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) one of which is nonzero, we can produce a GCD following the Euclidean algorithm below.
    🔗
    If \(\alpha\mid \beta\text{,}\) then \(\alpha\) is a GCD of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{.}\) Otherwise, we can produce a sequence of division algorithm instances
    \begin{align*} \beta\amp =q_1\alpha+r_1 \amp r_1\amp \ne 0, N(r_1)< N(\alpha) \\ \alpha\amp =q_2r_1+r_2 \amp r_2\amp \ne 0, N(r_2)< N(r_1)\\ \amp \vdots\\ r_{n-1} \amp =q_{n+1}r_{n}+r_{n+1} \amp r_{n+1}\amp\ne 0, N(r_{n+1})< N(r_{n})\\ r_{n} \amp = q_{n+2}r_{n+1}+\boxed{0}\text{,} \end{align*}
    and it follows that \(r_{n+1}\) is a GCD of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\text{.}\) Moreover, beginning with the penultimate equation \(r_{n-1}=q_{n+1}r_n+r_{n+1}\text{,}\) and working our way upward, solving successively for \(r_{k}\) in terms of \(r_{k-1}\) and \(r_{k-2}\text{,}\) we can find elements \(\gamma, \delta\in R\) satisfying
    \begin{align*} r_{n+1} \amp =\gamma\alpha+\delta\beta\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First note that if \(\beta=q\alpha+r\) for some \(q,r\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) then \(\delta\) is a GCD of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) if and only if it is a GCD of \(\alpha\) and \(r\text{,}\) using the usual divisibility arguments.
    🔗
    It follows by induction that if we have a sequence of division algorithm instances as in (2), then \(\delta\) is a GCD of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) if and only if it is a GCD of \(\) It suffices, then, to show that there is such a sequence. The basic idea is that at each step
    \begin{align*} r_{k} \amp =q_{k+2}r_{k+1}+r_{k+2}\text{,} \end{align*}
    if \(r_{k+2}\ne 0\text{,}\) then we can apply the division algorithm to \(r_{k+2}\) and \(r_{k+1}\text{.}\) Since at each step where the remainder is nonzero, the successive remainder has strictly smaller \(N\)-value, the process must terminate: i.e., at some point we must have \(r_{k}=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.13.6. GCD in \(\Z[i]\).

    Find a GCD \(\delta\) of \(2\) and \(1+3i\) in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) and write \(\delta=2\alpha+(1+3i)\beta\) for some \(\alpha, \beta\in \Z[i]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Noting that \(N(2)=4\) and \(N(1+3i)=10\text{,}\) we start the Euclidean algorithm by dividing \(1+3i\) by \(2\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} 1+3i \amp =(1+i)2+(-1+i)\\ 2 \amp =(-1+i)(-1-i)+\boxed{0}\text{.} \end{align*}
    We conclude that \(z=-1+i\) is a GCD of \(2\) and \(1+3i\text{.}\) Furthermore, we have
    \begin{align*} (-1+i) \amp =-(1+i)2+(1+3i)\text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 1.13.7. Bezout’s theorem for \(\Z[i]\).

    For any two elements \(\alpha, \beta\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) one of which is nonzero, there is a GCD \(\delta\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) Furthermore, we can write
    \begin{align} \delta \amp =\gamma \alpha+\omega \beta\tag{1.13.1} \end{align}
    for elements \(\gamma, \omega\in \Z[i]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 1.13.8. Relatively prime factors.

    Assume \(\alpha,\beta\) are relatively prime elements of \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
    1. If \(\alpha\mid \beta\gamma\text{,}\) then \(\alpha\mid \gamma\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(\alpha\mid \gamma\) and \(\beta\mid \gamma\text{,}\) then \(\alpha\beta\mid \gamma\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are relatively prime, we can write \(1=\delta \alpha+\omega\beta\) some \(\delta, \omega\in R\text{.}\) We will make use of this expression in both parts below.
    1. Assume that \(\alpha\mid \beta\gamma\) for some \(\gamma in R\text{.}\) By definition this means \(\beta\gamma=\epsilon \alpha\) for some \(\epsilon\in R\text{.}\) It follows that
      \begin{align} \gamma \amp =\gamma (\delta\alpha+\omega\beta)\tag{1.13.2}\\ \amp = (\gamma\delta)\alpha+\omega \ beta\gamma\tag{1.13.3}\\ \amp = (\gamma\delta+\omega\epsilon)\alpha\text{,}\tag{1.13.4} \end{align}
      and thus that \(\alpha\mid \gamma\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Assume that \(\alpha\mid \gamma\) and \(\beta\mid \gamma\) for some \(\gamma in R\text{.}\) By definition, this means \(\gamma=\epsilon \alpha\) and \(\gamma=\phi \beta\) for some \(\epsilon, \phi\in R\text{.}\) From \(1=\delta\alpha+\omega\beta\) it now follows that
      \begin{align*} \gamma \amp =\gamma\delta\alpha+\gamma\omega\beta\\ \amp = (\delta\phi)\alpha\beta+(\gamma\epsilon)\alpha\beta\text{,} \end{align*}
      and we see that \(\alpha\beta\mid \gamma\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.13.2 Primes and irreducibles in \(\Z[i]\)

    Definition 1.13.9. Primes and irreducibles.

    Let \(R\) be a subring of \(\C\text{,}\) and let \(\alpha\) be a nonzero non-unit of \(R\text{:}\) i.e., \(\alpha\in R-(R^*\cup \{0\})\text{.}\)
    • \(\alpha\) is irreducible if \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\) implies \(\beta\in R^*\) \(\gamma\in R^*\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\alpha\) is prime if \(\alpha\mid \beta\gamma\) implies \(\alpha\mid \beta\) or \(\alpha\mid \gamma\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.13.10. Disentangling primes and irreducibles.

    The two separate definitions above might come as a surprise to you, since in the context of \(\Z\) the two are equivalent! For a general subring \(R\subseteq \C\text{,}\) however, this is not the case. Although it is the case that all prime elements are irreducible, there are rings with irreducible elements that are not prime. Thankfully for us, in \(\Z[i]\) as in \(\Z\text{,}\) the two notions are equivalent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.13.11. Prime equivalent to irreducible in \(\Z[i]\).

    Let \(\pi\in \Z[i]\) be a nonzero non-unit. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \(\pi\) is prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\pi\) is irreducible.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The easier implication is (1)\(\implies\)(2); and indeed this implication holds for any subring \(R\subseteq \C\text{.}\) Assume \(\pi\) is prime. If \(\pi=\alpha\beta\text{,}\) then in particular \(\pi\mid \alpha\beta\text{.}\) Since \(\pi\) is prime, we have \(\pi\mid \alpha\) or \(\pi\mid \beta\text{.}\) If \(\pi\mid \alpha\text{,}\) then we have \(\alpha=\pi\gamma\) for some \(\gamma\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) and hence
    \begin{equation*} \pi=\alpha\beta=\pi\gamma\beta\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\pi\) is nonzero, it has a multiplicative inverse in \(\C\text{,}\) allowing us to cancel and conclude \(1=\gamma\beta\text{.}\) Since \(\gamma\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\beta\in (\Z[i])^*\text{.}\) The case where \(\pi\mid \beta\) is exactly similar.
    🔗
    The implication (2)\(\implies\)(1) is not true for a general subring \(R\subseteq \C\text{.}\) As you will see, the key property of \(\Z[i]\) we use is the existence of GCDs and Bezout’s theorem. Assume \(\pi\) is irreducible, and that \(\pi\mid \alpha\beta\) for \(\alpha,\beta\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) We wish to show that \(\pi\mid \alpha\) or \(\pi\mid \beta\text{.}\) To this end, assume that \(\pi\nmid \alpha\text{.}\) We claim that \(\pi\) and \(\alpha\) are relatively prime in this case. Indeed, since \(\pi\) is irreducible, its only divisors are units and associates of itself. It follows that a common divisor of \(\pi\) and \(\alpha\) would be a unit or an associate of \(\pi\text{.}\) But since \(\pi\nmid \alpha\text{,}\) no associate of \(\pi\) divides \(\alpha\text{.}\) We conclude that the only common divisors of \(\pi\) and \(\alpha\) are units, and hence that the two are are relatively prime. From Corollary 1.13.8 we conclude that \(\pi\mid \beta\text{,}\) as desired.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.13.12. Prime/irreducible.

    From here on out, we are justified in using the terms “prime” and “irreducible” interchangeably when working in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) For the sheer sake of brevity, as well as its familiar ring, we will prefer the term “prime”. However, when emphasizing the fact the two notions in Definition 1.13.9 are equivalent in this setting, we will sometimes write “prime/irreducible”.
    🔗
    🔗
    We are nearly prepared to prove a prime factorization theorem for \(\Z[i]\) that is a straightforward generalization of the corresponding result for \(\Z\text{.}\) The existence of prime factorizations will proceed by induction on the Gaussian norm of an element, and for the base case, we will need the following useful result.
    🔗

    Proposition 1.13.13. Primes, norms, and conjugates.

    Let \(\pi\in \Z[i]\) be a nonzero non-unit.
    1. \(\pi\) is prime if and only if \(\overline{\pi}\) is prime if and only if \(u\pi\) is prime for any unit \(u\in \Z[i]^*\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(N(\pi)=p\) for \(p\) a prime integer, then \(\pi\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(\pi\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) then \(N(\pi)\in \{p,p^2\}\text{,}\) where \(p\) is a prime integer.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. Proof by delegation. (In other words, this is left to you!) The first equivalence follows easily from the fact that \(\overline{\alpha\beta}=\overline{\alpha}\overline{\beta}\text{.}\) The second follows from the fact that units \(u\) can be cancelled multiplicatively in equations involving Gaussian integers.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Assume \(N(\pi)=p\) where \(p\) is a prime integer. We will show \(\pi\) is irreducible, hence prime. Given \(\alpha,\beta\in \Z[i]\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \pi=\alpha\beta \amp \implies p=N(\pi)=N(\alpha)N(\beta)\\ \amp \implies \{N(\alpha),N(\beta)\}=\{1,p\} \amp (p \text{ prime})\\ \amp \implies N(\alpha)=1 \text{ or } N(\beta)=1\\ \amp \implies \alpha\in \Z[i]^* \text{ or } \beta\in \Z[i]^*\text{.} \end{align*}
      Thus \(\pi\) is irreducible/prime, as claimed.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Assume \(\pi\) is prime/irreducible. Write \(a=N(\pi)\text{,}\) where \(a\) is an integer and \(a> 1\) (since \(\pi\) is not unit). By prime factorization, we can write \(a=\prod_{i=1}^r p_i\) for primes \(p_i\text{.}\) Since \(\pi\mid a\) in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) it divides \(p_i\) for some \(i\text{.}\) Call \(p=p_i\text{.}\) Since \(\pi\mid p\text{,}\) we have \(p=\pi\alpha\) for some \(\alpha\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) Taking conjugates, we have
      \begin{align*} p\amp=\overline{p}=\overline{\pi}\overline{\alpha} \amp \text{.} \end{align*}
      But then
      \begin{align*} p^2 \amp =pp\\ \amp =\pi\overline{\pi}\alpha\overline{\alpha}\\ \amp = N(\pi)N(\alpha)\text{.} \end{align*}
      Since \(p\) is prime the only factors of \(p^2\) are \(1,p,p^2\text{.}\) Since \(N(\pi)\ne 1\text{,}\) we conclude that \(N(\pi)\in\{p,p^2\}\text{,}\) as claimed.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.13.14. Gaussian primes.

    For each Gaussian integer \(\alpha\) verify directly whether it is prime or not, and compare your answer with \(N(\alpha)\text{.}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \alpha=1+i\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \alpha=1-i\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle \alpha=3\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle \alpha=-1+3i\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Note that \(N(1+i)=N(1-i)=2\text{,}\) which implies (indirectly) that these elements are both prime. To verify this directly, it is easiest to show that \(\alpha\) in this case is irreducible, and this is done using a norm argument identical to the one used in the proof of Proposition 1.13.13. Note that \(1-i=\overline{1+i}\text{,}\) and thus the primality of either element implies the primality of the other. Interestingly, we also have \(1-i=(-i)(1+i)\text{,}\) so these two primes are in fact associates of each other. More on that later. Lastly, the nontrivial factorization \(2=(1+i)(1-i)\) shows that \(2\text{,}\) though prime in \(\Z\text{,}\) is not prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We claim \(3\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{.}\) If \(3=\alpha\beta\text{,}\) then \(9=N(3)=N(\alpha)N(\beta)\text{.}\) Since the divisors of \(9\) are \(1,3,9\text{,}\) we see that either \(\{N(\alpha),N(\beta)\}=\{3\}\text{,}\) or \(\{N(\alpha), N(\beta)\}=\{1,9\}\text{.}\) The first option is impossible, as there are no integers \(a,b\in \Z\) satisfying \(3=a^2+b^2\text{.}\) The latter implies that \(\alpha\) is a unit or \(\beta\) is a unit, as desired. Note in this case that \(3\) is prime in \(\Z[i]\text{,}\) but its norm \(N(3)=9\) is not prime. It is, however, equal to the square of a prime, as predicted by Proposition 1.13.13.
    🔗
    We first compute \(N(\alpha)=1+9=10\text{.}\) Since \(10\) is not \(p\) or \(p^2\) for any prime integer \(p\text{,}\) we must have \(\alpha\) not prime/irreducible. To verify this directly, note that if \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\) is a nontrivial factorization, then \(\{N(\beta),N(\gamma)\}=\{2,5\}\text{.}\) This tells us to look for factors of \(\alpha\) with norm \(2\) and \(5\text{,}\) which leads us to the nontrivial factorization \(-1+3i=(1+i)(1+2i)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    We are ready at last for our prime factorization theorem for \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 1.13.15. Prime factorization in \(\Z[i]\).

    Let \(\alpha\in \Z[i]\) be a nonzero non-unit element.
    1. Prime factorization.
      There are prime/irreducible elements \(\pi_1,\pi_2,\dots, \pi_r\) of \(\Z[i]\) satisfying
      \begin{align} \alpha \amp =\pi_1\pi_2\cdots \pi_r\text{.}\tag{1.13.5} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Uniqueness of factorization.
      If we have
      \begin{equation*} \alpha=\pi_1\pi_2\cdots \pi_r=\pi_1'\pi_2'\cdots \pi_s'\text{,} \end{equation*}
      with \(\pi_i\) and \(\pi_j'\) prime/irreducible for all \(i\) and \(j\text{,}\) then \(r=s\text{,}\) and after a reordering, for all \(i\) we have \(\pi_i'=u_i\pi_i\) for some unit \(u_i\in (\Z[i])^*=\{1,-1,i,-i\}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      In particular, the number of irreducible factors in an irreducible factorization of \(\alpha\) is unique, and the irreducible factors are unique up to multiplication by a unit (i.e., up to associates).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. The proof is by strong induction on $N(\alpha)$, the base case being \(N(\alpha)=2\text{.}\) (We cannot have \(N(\alpha)\in \{0,1\}\text{,}\) since \(\alpha\) by assumption is a nonzero non-unit.) If $N(\alpha)=2$, then \(\alpha\) is itself prime by Proposition 1.13.13, and we are done.
      🔗
      For the strong induction step, we assume the result is true for all Gaussian integers with norm in \(\{2,3,\dots, n-1\}\text{.}\) Assume \(N(\alpha)=n\text{.}\) If \(\alpha\) is prime/irreducible, we are done. Otherwise, we can write \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\) for some non-units \(\beta,\gamma\in \Z[i]\text{.}\) Since \(n=N(\alpha)=N(\beta)N(\gamma)\text{,}\) and since \(N(\beta),N(\gamma)> 1\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\{N(\beta),N(\gamma)\}\subseteq \{2,3,\dots, n-1\}\text{.}\) By induction we have
      \begin{align*} \beta \amp= \pi_1\pi_2\cdots \pi_r \amp \gamma \amp= \pi_1'\pi_2'\cdots \pi_s' \end{align*}
      for primes \(\pi_i, \pi_j'\text{.}\) But then
      \begin{equation*} \alpha=\beta\gamma=\pi_1\cdots \pi_r\pi_1'\cdots \pi_s' \end{equation*}
      is a prime factorization of \(\alpha\text{,}\) as desired.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The uniqueness claim is proved by induction on the minimal length \(r\) of a prime factorization.
      🔗
      Base step: \(r=1\text{.}\) In this case we have \(\alpha\) itself prime/irreducible. Suppose \(\alpha=\prod_{i=1}^s\pi_i\) is another prime factorization. If \(s> 1\text{,}\) then we would be able to write \(\alpha=\pi_1 \cdot \beta\text{,}\) where \(\pi\) and \(\beta=\prod_{i=2}^s\pi_i\) are both non-units, contradicting the irreducibility of \(\alpha\text{.}\) Thus \(s=1\text{,}\) and we have \(\alpha=\pi_1\text{.}\) This proves the uniqueness claim for the case \(r=1\) when \(\alpha\) is itself prime.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Induction step: we assume that for any \(\beta\in \Z[i]\) with a prime factorization of minimal length \(r\geq 1\text{,}\) the factorization is unique in the manner described. Suppose \(\alpha\) has a prime factorization of minimal length \(r+1\) of the form
      \begin{equation*} \alpha=\prod_{i=1}^{r+1}\pi_i \end{equation*}
      and suppose \(\alpha=\prod_{i=1}^{s} \pi_i'\) is another prime factorization. Since \(r\) is minimal, we must have \(s\geq r\text{.}\) Since \(\pi_1\mid \alpha=\prod_{i=1}^s\pi_i'\text{,}\) we must have \(\pi_1\mid \pi_{i_0}'\) for some \(1\leq i_{0}\leq s\text{.}\) After a re-ordering, we may assume \(i_0=1\text{.}\) We then have \(\pi_{1}'=u_1\pi_1\) for some unit \(u\in \Z[i]^*\text{.}\) This allows us to cancel \(\pi_1\) from both sides of the equation, yielding
      \begin{equation*} \beta=\prod_{i=2}^{r+1}\pi_i=\prod_{i=2}^s\pi_i'\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(\beta\) has a prime factorization of minimal length (at most) \(r\text{,}\) induction applies.It follows that \(r=s-1\text{,}\) and after a reordering, \(\pi_i'=u_i\pi_i\) for some unit \(u_i\in \Z[i]^*\) for all \(i\geq 2\text{.}\) But then we have \(r+1=s\) and \(\pi_i'=u_i\pi_i\) for all \(1\leq i\leq r+1\text{,}\) as desired.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    As with factorization in \(\Z\text{,}\) from a prime factorization of an element \(\alpha\text{,}\) we can produce a prime power factorization by grouping together “like” primes, which in this case means primes that are associate. For example, from the prime factorization
    \begin{align} 6 \amp = (1+i)(1-i)3\text{,}\tag{1.13.6} \end{align}
    observing that \(1-i=(-i)(1+i)\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} 6 \amp =(-i)(1+i)^2 3\text{,} \end{align*}
    expressing \(6\) as a product of a unit and powers of two non-associate primes. The corollary below gives the general result. The proof is very similar to the analogous result for integers and is left to the reader.
    🔗

    Corollary 1.13.16. Prime power factorization in \(\Z[i]\).

    Let \(\alpha\) be a nonzero non-unit element of \(\Z[i]\text{.}\)
    1. Prime power factorization.
      There are pairwise non-associate prime/irreducible elements \(\pi_1,\pi_2,\dots, \pi_r\in \Z[i]\text{,}\)positive integers \(n_1,n_2,\dots, n_r\text{,}\) and a unit \(u\in \Z[i]^*\) such that
      \begin{align} \alpha \amp =u\prod_{i=1}^r\pi_i^{n_i}\text{.}\tag{1.13.7} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Uniqueness of factorization.
      If
      \begin{align} \alpha\amp= u'\prod_{i=1}^s(\pi_i')^{m_i} \tag{1.13.8} \end{align}
      is another such factorization into powers of pairwise nonassociate primes \(\pi_i'\text{,}\) then \(r=s\text{,}\) and after a reordering, \(\pi_i'\) is associate to \(\pi_i\) and \(n_i=m_i\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
