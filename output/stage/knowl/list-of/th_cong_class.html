Theorem 1.1.4. Congruence classes.

Fix a modulus \(n\text{.}\)
  1. The following statements are equivalent.
    1. \([a]_n=[b]_n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(a\in [b]_n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(a\equiv b\pmod n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
  2. The congruence classes modulo \(n\) form a partition of \(\Z\text{.}\) In more detail, we have
    \begin{equation*} \Z=\bigcup_{a\in \Z}[a]_n\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and if \([a]_n\ne [b]_n\text{,}\) then \([a]_n\cap [b]_n=\emptyset\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  3. Given any \(a\in \Z\text{,}\) writing \(a=qn+r\) with \(0\leq r\leq n-1\) as in the division algorithm, we have \([a]_n=[r]_n\text{.}\) Furthermore, \([i]_n\ne [j]_n\) for any \(0\leq i\ne j\leq n-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As a result, we have
    \begin{align} \Z/n\Z \amp = \{[0]_n, [1]_n, \dots, [n-1]_n\}\tag{1.1.1} \end{align}
    and
    \begin{align} \abs{\Z/n\Z} \amp = n\text{.}\tag{1.1.2} \end{align}
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
🔗

Proof.

The theorem is a consequence of properties of the congruence relation learned earlier, and general properties about equivalence relations.
