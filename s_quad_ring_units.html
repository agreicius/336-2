    Section 1.16 Unit groups of quadratic extensions

    Theorem 1.16.1. Unit groups of imaginary quadratic extensions.

    Let \(d\) be a negative squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
    • If \(d=-1\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{O}_K^*=\{1,-1,i,-i\}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(d=-3\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{O}_K=\{1,\omega, \dots, \omega^5\}\text{,}\) where \(\omega=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i=e^{\pi i/3}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • If \(d=-2\) or \(d\leq -5\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{O}_K^*=\{1,-1\}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.16.2.

    It is worth pointing out a notational trick employed in the proof above that allows us to avoid the annoyance of dealing with the different descriptions of \(\mathcal{O}_K\) depending on whether or not \(d\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{.}\) Namely, we make the simple observation that in all cases (\(d\equiv 1,2,3\pmod 4\)), an arbitrary element of \(\mathcal{O}_K\) can be written as
    \begin{align*} \alpha \amp =\frac{m}{2}+\frac{n}{2}\sqrt{d} \end{align*}
    for some integers \(m\) and \(n\text{.}\) Of course, it is not the case that any choice of integers \(m\) and \(n\) will produce an element of \(\mathcal{O}_K\) in this way. In fact, if \(d\equiv 2,3\pmod 4\text{,}\) then we must have \(m\) and \(n\) even; and if \(d\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{,}\) we must have \(m\equiv n\pmod 2\text{.}\) However, for many of the arguments below, the exact nature of of the coefficients in our expression of \(\alpha\) is not important. When this is the case, we will simply write
    \begin{equation*} \alpha=q_1+q_2\mathcal{d}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    with the assumption that \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are at worst half-integers.
    🔗
    🔗
    We see then that the story of the unit groups of imaginary quadratic rings of integers is quite a simple one. Things are significantly more complicated for the real quadratic extensions.
    🔗

    Example 1.16.3. Units of \(\Z[\sqrt{2}]\).

    Let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) Show that \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\) is infinite.
    🔗
    Solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.16.4. Unit group of real quadratic extensions.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a positive squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\)
    1. There is a unit \(u\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\) such that \(u\ne \pm 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The set \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\) has a least element \(\epsilon_d\text{,}\) and we have
      \begin{align} \mathcal{O}_K^* \amp =\{\pm \epsilon_d^n\mid n\in \Z\}\text{.}\tag{1.16.1} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.16.5. Fundamental unit.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a positive squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) A fundamental unit of \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is an element \(\epsilon\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\) such that \(\mathcal{O}_K^*=\{\pm \epsilon^n\mid n\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We defer the proof of (1) of Theorem 1.16.4 until the next section. That argument will involve an analysis of the Pell equation \(x^2-dy^2=1\text{.}\) For now, we will content ourselves with showing how (1) implies (2). For this, the following lemma will come in handy.
    🔗

    Lemma 1.16.6. Units in \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\).

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a positive squarefree integer, and let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{.}\) Assume \(u=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\in \mathcal{O}_K^*-\{1,-1\}\text{.}\)
    1. We have
      \begin{align} u \amp \in (-\infty, -1)\cup (-1,0)\cup (0,1)\cup (1,\infty) \text{.}\tag{1.16.2} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We have
      \begin{align} \{u,-u,u^{-1},-u^{-1}\} \amp = \{q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}, q_1-q_2\sqrt{d},-q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}, -q_1-q_2\sqrt{d}\}\subseteq \mathcal{O}_K^* \text{.}\tag{1.16.3} \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Each interval appearing in (1.16.2) contains exactly one of the elements \(\{u,-u,u^{-1},-u^{-1}\}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(u\in (1,\infty)\) if and only if \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are positive.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. Since \(d> 0\text{,}\) \(K\subseteq \R\text{.}\) Since \(u\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\text{,}\) we must have \(u\ne 0\text{.}\) And by assumption we have \(u\ne \pm 1\text{.}\) It follows that \(\alpha\in \R-\{-1,1,0\}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Clearly \(u\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\) if and only if \(-u\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\text{.}\) Furthermore, since \(u\in \mathcal{O}_K\) if and only if \(\overline{u}\in \mathcal{O}_K\text{,}\) and since \(u^{-1}=\pm \overline{u}\text{,}\) we have \(u\in \mathcal{O}_K\) if and only if \(u^{-1}\in \mathcal{O}_K\text{.}\) Lastly, the set equality follows easily from our formula for inverses and the fact that \(N_K(u)=\pm 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. In general, for any \(\alpha\in \R-\{-1,0,1\}\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{align*} \alpha\in (1,\infty) \amp\iff \alpha^{-1}\in (0,1) \\ \amp \iff -\alpha^{-1}\in (-1, 0)\\ \amp \iff -\alpha\in (-\infty,-1) \text{,} \end{align*}
      and thus each of the four intervals of (1.16.2) contains exactly one of the elements of \(\{u, u{-1},-u^{-1},-u\}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. First note that since \(N_K(u)=q_1^2-q_2^2d=\pm 1\) and \(d\geq 2\text{,}\) we cannot have \(q_1=0\text{;}\) and since \(u\ne \pm 1\text{,}\) we cannot have \(q_2=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Next, since \(u'=\abs{q_1}+\abs{q_2}\sqrt{d}\) is clearly the maximum element of \(\{u,-u,u^{-1},-u^{-1}\}\text{,}\) and since exactly one of these elements lies in \((1,\infty)\text{,}\) \(u'\) must be the sole element of \((1,\infty)\text{.}\) It follows that \(u\in (1,\infty)\) if and only if \(u=u'\) if and only if \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are positive.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    And now for the proof that the existence of a nontrivial unit implies the existence of a fundamental unit.
    🔗

    Proof that (1) implies (2) in Theorem 1.16.4.

    Assume we have \(u=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\in \mathcal{O}_K^*-\{1,-1\}\text{.}\) From Lemma 1.16.6 we may assume \(q_1\) and \(q_2\) are both positive and thus that \(u\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Finding the smallest element of \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\) might actually be tricky, since we are comparing sizes of irrational elements. The next corollary simplifies things for us significantly: for elements \(u=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\) of \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\text{,}\) we can compare size simply by looking at the second coefficient \(q_2\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Corollary 1.16.7. Unit group of real quadratic extensions.

    Let \(d\in \Z-\{0,1\}\) be a positive squarefree integer, let \(K=\Q(\sqrt{d})\text{,}\) and let \(u=q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\) and \(u'=q_1'+q_2'\sqrt{d}\) be elements of \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\text{.}\) We have \(u< u'\) if and only if \(q_1< q_1'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As a result, the fundamental unit \(\epsilon_d\) described in Theorem 1.16.4 is the element \(q_1+q_2\sqrt{d}\) of \(\mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\) with minimal first coefficient \(q_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\epsilon\) be the smallest element of \(\mathcal{O}_K\cap(1,\infty)\text{,}\) and write \(\epsilon=r+s\sqrt{d}\text{,}\) where \(r=m/2\) and \(s=n/2\) for some positive integers \(m,n\in \Z\text{.}\) Since \(\mathcal{O}_K^*=\{\pm \epsilon^n\mid n\in \Z\}\text{,}\) and since \(\abs{\epsilon^{n}}\leq 1\) for all \(n\leq 0 \text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)=\{\epsilon^n\mid n\geq 1\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since furthermore the function \(f(x)=\epsilon^x\) is increasing, we have
    \begin{equation*} \epsilon< \epsilon^2< \epsilon^3< \dots\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus for all \(u,v\in \mathcal{O}_K^*\cap (1,\infty)\text{,}\) we have \(u=\epsilon^m\) and \(v=\epsilon^n\) for some positive integers \(m\) and \(n\text{,}\) and \(u< v\) if and only if \(m< n\text{.}\) Let \(\epsilon^n=r_n+s_n\sqrt{d}\text{.}\) To finish the proof, it suffices to show that \(r_n< r_{n+1}\) for all \(n\geq 1\text{,}\) or equivalently, that \(r_n\) is an increasing function of \(n\text{.}\) Since
    \begin{align*} \epsilon^{n+1} \amp =\epsilon \epsilon^{n}\\ \amp =(r_1+s_1\sqrt{d})(r_n+s_n\sqrt{d})\\ \amp =(r_1r_n+s_1s_nd)+(r_1s_n+r_ns_1)\sqrt{d}\text{,} \end{align*}
    we see that
    \begin{align*} r_{n+1} \amp = r_1r_n+s_1s_nd \amp s_{n+1}\amp=r_1s_n+r_ns_1\text{.} \end{align*}
    Recall that since \(\epsilon\in (1,\infty)\text{,}\) we have \(r_1,s_1> 0\text{.}\) If \(r_1> 1\text{,}\) then \(r_{n+1}> r_n+s_1s_nd> r_n\text{.}\) The only other option is \(r_1=\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) In this case we must have \(d\equiv 1\pmod 4\text{.}\) Furthermore, since
    \begin{align*} s_1^2d=r_1^2\pm 1=\frac{1}{4}\pm 1 \amp \text{,} \end{align*}
    we must have \(s_1^2d=1/4+1=5/4\text{,}\) from whence it follows that \(d\leq 5\text{.}\) But then \(d=5\) (since \(d\equiv 1\pmod 4\)), and we have \(s_1=1/2\text{.}\) Now we are in the very special case where \(\epsilon=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{5}}{2}\text{.}\) Since \(\epsilon\) satisfies \(f(x)=(x-\epsilon)(x-\overline{\epsilon})=x^2-x-1\text{,}\) it follows that \(\epsilon^2=\epsilon+1\text{,}\) and by induction \(\epsilon^{n+1}=\epsilon^{n}+\epsilon^{n-1}\) for all \(n\geq 2\text{.}\) As a result, looking only at the first coefficients of these powers, we have \(r_{n+1}=r_{n}+r_{n-1}\) for all \(n\) (and \(s_{n+1}=s_n+s_{n-1}\)). Since \(r_k> 0\) for all \(k\geq 1\text{,}\) we see that \(r_{n+1}> r_n\text{,}\) as desired.
